# Canonization

One of the most persistent myths about early Christianity is that a powerful church council — usually identified as the Council of Nicaea (325 AD) — sat down, reviewed the available Christian writings, voted on which ones were "in" and which were "out," and thereby created the Bible as we know it. The implication is that competing gospels were suppressed, that the canon was a political product, and that our New Testament is missing significant voices.

This narrative is popular. It appears in Dan Brown's *The Da Vinci Code*, in casual conversation, and sometimes in academic work. It is also, as a historical account of what happened, substantially false.

## What Nicaea Did and Did Not Do

The Council of Nicaea (325 AD) was convened by Emperor Constantine to address a specific theological controversy: the Arian heresy, which held that Jesus was a created being ("there was a time when he was not") rather than coeternal with the Father. The council addressed this question, produced the Nicene Creed, set the date of Easter, and handled various matters of church discipline.

**It did not address the canon of Scripture.** There is no record in any of the Council's surviving documents — or in the contemporary accounts of those present, including Eusebius of Caesarea and Athanasius of Alexandria — of a vote on which books belonged in the Bible. <Footnote id="bruce-canon" source="Bruce, *The Canon of Scripture*, pp. 97–99." />

The claim that Nicaea established the biblical canon is not a scholarly finding. It is an error, and a traceable one.

## How the Canon Actually Formed

The formation of the canon was a gradual process of recognition rather than a single act of selection. Several categories of evidence are relevant:

### Early Usage

By the late 2nd century — 150 years before Nicaea — church leaders were quoting the same core set of texts as authoritative: the four Gospels, Paul's letters, Acts, and portions of what we now call the Catholic Epistles. Irenaeus of Lyon (~180 AD) explicitly argues for a four-gospel canon, which he treats as settled, not in dispute. <Footnote id="irenaeus-adv-haer" source="Irenaeus, *Adversus Haereses* III.11.8 (c. 180 AD)." />

### Criteria for Recognition

When early Christians debated which writings were authoritative, they employed identifiable criteria:

**Apostolicity**: Was the writing associated with an apostle or someone with firsthand access to the apostles? This is why the four Gospels — Matthew (apostle), Mark (Peter's associate), Luke (Paul's associate), and John (apostle) — were universally received, while the Gospel of Thomas, Gospel of Philip, and similar writings were not.

**Catholicity**: Was the writing read and accepted across the geographically diverse church, not just in one community?

**Orthodoxy**: Did the writing's content cohere with what Christians everywhere recognized as the apostolic message?

**Antiquity**: Did the writing date to the apostolic period?

### The Muratorian Fragment

The Muratorian Fragment, dating to approximately 170–200 AD (though some scholars argue for a later date), is the earliest known list of canonical writings. <Footnote id="mcdonald-canon" source="McDonald, *The Biblical Canon*, pp. 369–378. The date of the Muratorian Fragment is debated; McDonald and others discuss the range of scholarly opinion." /> It lists most of the New Testament books we have today and specifically excludes writings the community considered spurious.

### Development in the 4th Century

By the 4th century, there was broad agreement on the core canon. The disputed books were primarily at the margins: Hebrews (authorship uncertain), James, 2 Peter, 2–3 John, Jude, Revelation. Athanasius's Easter Letter of 367 AD is the first document to list exactly the 27 books of the New Testament as we have it today. <Footnote id="bruce-canon" source="Bruce, p. 209." />

Councils did eventually ratify the canon — notably the Council of Hippo (393 AD) and the Council of Carthage (397 AD). But these councils were *recognizing* what had already emerged from usage, not *creating* something new.

## The Gnostic Gospels

The Gospel of Thomas, Gospel of Philip, Gospel of Mary Magdalene, and similar writings discovered at Nag Hammadi, Egypt, in 1945 are sometimes presented as suppressed voices that a power-hungry church silenced.

The historical reality is more complex:

**Dating**: The Gnostic gospels, in their surviving form, date from the 2nd–4th centuries AD — later than the canonical Gospels. The Gospel of Thomas in particular may preserve some early traditions, but in its full form reflects 2nd-century Gnostic theology.

**Character**: The Gnostic texts were not suppressed accounts of a Jewish carpenter from Galilee. They reflect Gnostic theology — a quite different religious worldview emphasizing spiritual knowledge (*gnosis*), the material world as evil, and a secret tradition accessible only to initiates. The "Jesus" of the Gospel of Philip or the Gospel of Mary is a very different figure from the Jesus of Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John. <Footnote id="ehrman-lost-christianities" source="Ehrman, *Lost Christianities*, pp. 52–78." />

**Non-suppression**: Many Gnostic texts were known to early Christian writers like Irenaeus, Tertullian, and Origen. They were not suppressed secrets — they were publicly discussed, debated, and rejected as not reflecting the apostolic teaching.

Elaine Pagels, whose *The Gnostic Gospels* (1979) is sympathetic to the Gnostic writings, does not argue that they were part of an original apostolic tradition. Her argument is that they represent a valuable alternative form of early Christianity. <Footnote id="pagels-gnostic" source="Pagels, *The Gnostic Gospels*, Random House, 1979." /> That is a legitimate position to argue, but it is not the same as the popular claim that they were suppressed because they contained embarrassing truths.

## Conclusion

The formation of the biblical canon was a complex historical process that unfolded over several centuries. It was not a single council vote, and it was not primarily a political act of suppression. It was a process of recognition — the early Christian communities identifying, across geographical and cultural distances, which writings they believed carried apostolic authority and which did not.

Whether those communities made the right call is a theological and historical question that deserves serious engagement. But the *mechanism* of canon formation — gradual recognition by usage and criteria, not top-down imposition by Constantine — is not seriously disputed among historians of early Christianity.
