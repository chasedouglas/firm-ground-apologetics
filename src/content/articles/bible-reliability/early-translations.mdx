# Early Translations

The Greek New Testament was not read only in Greek. Within decades of the original composition, the texts were being translated into the languages of the communities where Christianity spread. These ancient translations — called "versions" in the scholarly literature — are another form of manuscript evidence, often preserving text types that predate our surviving Greek manuscripts.

## A Common Misconception

Before examining the versions, we should address a persistent misconception: the idea that modern Bibles are translations of translations of translations — that we are reading something like a photocopy of a photocopy, degraded at each step.

This misunderstands how modern Bible translation works.

Modern English Bible translations — the ESV, NIV, NASB, NET, and others — are translated **directly from the original Hebrew and Greek**, using critical editions based on the manuscript evidence discussed elsewhere in this section. The Masoretic Text and Biblia Hebraica Stuttgartensia for the Old Testament, and the Nestle-Aland Greek New Testament (currently in its 28th edition) for the New Testament.

Translation is *interpretation* — choices must be made about how to render Greek idioms into English — but it is not a game of telephone degrading the original. Every modern translation is one step from the reconstructed original text.

## The Latin Tradition

The Latin New Testament has two distinct phases:

**Old Latin** (*Vetus Latina*): A collection of early Latin translations produced in North Africa and Europe beginning in the late 2nd century AD. These are not a unified translation but various independent versions, some preserving very early text types. The Vetus Latina Institute in Beuron, Germany continues the scholarly project of cataloguing all known Old Latin manuscripts.

**The Vulgate**: Commissioned by Pope Damasus I and largely the work of Jerome between 382 and 405 AD, the Vulgate was a fresh translation from the best Greek and Hebrew texts Jerome could access. <Footnote id="metzger-early-versions" source="Metzger, *The Early Versions of the New Testament*, pp. 285–340." /> It became the standard Bible of the Western church for over a thousand years. Approximately 10,000 Latin Vulgate manuscripts survive — more than almost any other ancient manuscript tradition.

## The Syriac Tradition

Syriac, a dialect of Aramaic, was the language of Christianity in the eastern reaches of the Roman world. The Syriac versions of the New Testament include:

**Peshitta**: The standard Syriac Bible, whose New Testament portion dates in its current form to the early 5th century. "Peshitta" means "simple" or "common" — the Syriac equivalent of the Vulgate. It remains the Bible of Syriac-speaking Christian communities today.

**Diatessaron**: A harmony of the four Gospels compiled by Tatian c. 170 AD. Significant because it demonstrates that all four Gospels were known and considered authoritative in Syria by the late 2nd century.

**Old Syriac**: Earlier Syriac Gospels texts, surviving in two manuscripts (the Sinaitic Syriac and the Curetonian Syriac), dating to the late 4th–early 5th centuries but likely representing a 2nd–3rd century translation.

## The Coptic Tradition

Coptic (descended from ancient Egyptian) was the language of Egyptian Christianity. The Coptic versions — particularly the Sahidic (upper Egypt) and Bohairic (lower Egypt) dialects — date from the 3rd–4th centuries onward. The Coptic manuscripts are particularly valuable because they are geographically associated with the same region that produced the early Greek papyri, providing additional cross-reference points.

## The Armenian, Ethiopic, and Georgian Traditions

As Christianity spread into Armenia (early 4th century), Ethiopia (4th century), and Georgia (5th century), the Scriptures followed. These translations often depend on earlier Greek or Syriac exemplars and provide additional windows into the textual tradition:

**Armenian**: Translated in the early 5th century by Mesrop Mashtots and Sahak Parthev, who also created the Armenian alphabet for this purpose. Thousands of Armenian NT manuscripts survive.

**Ethiopic (Ge'ez)**: Translated c. 4th–6th centuries. The Ethiopian Orthodox Church preserves an extremely ancient manuscript tradition.

## How Versions Help Reconstruct the Text

The versions are valuable to textual critics in several ways:

**Dating**: A translation made in 200 AD presupposes a Greek exemplar that is at least that early. Where versions agree with Greek manuscripts against other Greek manuscripts, they help establish the antiquity of a reading.

**Independence**: A Latin manuscript and a Coptic manuscript translated from independent sources provide independent witnesses. Where they agree, they triangulate toward the original.

**Text types**: Different versions tend to preserve different textual traditions. Comparing them allows scholars to identify and date the major text types.

The limitation is translation itself: a translator makes choices that are not always reversible. You cannot always know whether a translation is preserving one reading or another, because both might translate identically. The versions supplement but do not replace Greek manuscript evidence.

## Conclusion

When someone says the Bible has "been translated many times," they are describing a history of extraordinary geographical spread — Christianity moving into Latin, Syriac, Coptic, Armenian, Ethiopic, and Georgian communities, each of which translated the text for their communities. This is evidence of early wide dissemination, not evidence of corruption.

The manuscript tradition underlying modern translations is Greek (for the NT) and Hebrew (for the OT). Modern Bibles are translations from those originals, informed by the richest documentary evidence of any ancient text.
