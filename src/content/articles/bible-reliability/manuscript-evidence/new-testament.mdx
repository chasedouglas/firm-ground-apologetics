# New Testament Manuscripts

The Greek New Testament manuscript tradition is catalogued by the Institut für Neutestamentliche Textforschung (INTF) at the University of Münster, Germany — the recognized international authority on New Testament manuscript scholarship. Their register, known as the *Kurzgefasste Liste*, divides manuscripts into four categories based on their physical form and use.

## The Four Categories

### Papyri

The oldest category — manuscripts written on papyrus, typically in roll or codex form. The INTF currently lists 140 designated papyri (designated with the letter *P* followed by a number). <Footnote id="intf-vmr" source="INTF, New Testament Virtual Manuscript Room. Current papyrus count as of 2023. The list is updated as new discoveries are catalogued." />

Papyrus was expensive and fragile; that so many survive is partly due to the dry climate of Egypt, where most were discovered. The earliest and most significant include:

**P52** — The John Rylands Papyrus. A small fragment of the Gospel of John (18:31–33, 37–38), dated c. 100–150 AD by most paleographers. Its recto and verso are both inscribed, indicating it came from a codex (book form) rather than a scroll — a notably early use of codex format. Held at the John Rylands Library, University of Manchester. [Digital facsimile available](https://www.library.manchester.ac.uk/rylands/special-collections/using-the-collections/papyri/).

{/* VERIFY: The date of P52 has been debated. The traditional date of c. 125 AD was challenged by Brent Nongbri (2005, 2010) who argued the paleographic dating is not as certain as typically claimed and that a date anywhere from 100-200 AD is defensible. Most scholars still prefer the earlier end of this range. Worth noting this scholarly debate. */}

**P46** — Chester Beatty Papyrus II. One of the most important early manuscripts, containing much of Paul's letters (Romans, Hebrews, 1–2 Corinthians, Ephesians, Galatians, Philippians, Colossians, 1 Thessalonians). Dated c. 175–225 AD. Parts held at the Chester Beatty Library, Dublin, and the University of Michigan. [Chester Beatty Library digital access](https://chesterbeatty.ie/collections/western-collections/biblical-and-early-christian-collections/).

**P66** — Bodmer Papyrus II. Contains most of the Gospel of John. Dated c. 200 AD. Now at the Bodmer Library (Fondation Martin Bodmer), Geneva.

**P75** — Bodmer Papyri XIV–XV. Significant portions of Luke and John. Dated c. 175–225 AD. Notable for its close agreement with Codex Vaticanus (a fourth-century codex), demonstrating that Vaticanus's text type is ancient, not a later editorial product. Transferred to the Vatican Apostolic Library in 2007.

### Uncials (Majuscules)

Manuscripts written in large, separate capital letters on parchment (treated animal skin), primarily from the 4th–9th centuries. The INTF lists 323 uncials. The most significant:

**Codex Sinaiticus (ℵ, or "01")** — Fourth century, c. 330–360 AD. Contains the complete New Testament — the oldest complete New Testament manuscript in existence — plus large portions of the Old Testament (in Greek, the Septuagint), the Epistle of Barnabas, and the Shepherd of Hermas. Discovered at St. Catherine's Monastery, Sinai, by Constantine von Tischendorf in the 19th century. Now divided between the British Library (347 leaves), Leipzig University Library, the National Library of Russia, and St. Catherine's Monastery. <Footnote id="codex-sinaiticus-project" source="Codex Sinaiticus Project. The manuscript is fully digitized and freely available at codexsinaiticus.org." /> The Codex Sinaiticus Project has produced a fully searchable, high-resolution digital edition: [codexsinaiticus.org](https://www.codexsinaiticus.org/).

**Codex Vaticanus (B, or "03")** — Fourth century, considered by many textual critics the single most important New Testament manuscript. Contains most of the Old and New Testaments in Greek. Held in the Vatican Apostolic Library since at least the 15th century. [Vatican digital catalogue](https://digi.vatlib.it/).

**Codex Alexandrinus (A, or "02")** — Fifth century. Contains most of the Bible plus 1–2 Clement. Held at the British Library. [British Library digitized](https://www.bl.uk/manuscripts/FullDisplay.aspx?ref=Royal_MS_1_D_VIII).

**Codex Bezae (D, or "05")** — Fifth century. Bilingual (Greek and Latin), contains the Gospels and Acts. Notable for its distinctive text type with numerous unique readings. Held at Cambridge University Library. [Digital version at Cambridge Digital Library](https://cudl.lib.cam.ac.uk/view/MS-NN-00002-00041).

**Codex Washingtonianus (W, or "032")** — Late 4th–early 5th century. Contains the four Gospels. Held at the Freer Gallery of Art, Smithsonian Institution, Washington, D.C.

### Minuscules

Manuscripts written in a smaller, connected script, primarily from the 9th century onward. The INTF lists 2,908 minuscules — the largest category. While later in date, minuscules are often copied from ancient exemplars and can preserve early text types. The CSNTM has digitized hundreds of previously unstudied minuscules in recent years. <Footnote id="csntm-digital" source="Center for the Study of New Testament Manuscripts. The CSNTM has photographed manuscripts in monasteries, libraries, and private collections around the world, making them available for the first time to international scholars." />

### Lectionaries

Manuscripts organized for liturgical reading rather than continuous text. The INTF lists 2,437 lectionaries. These are important for establishing the history of the text in church use, though they are sometimes less directly useful for reconstruction of the original text.

| Category | Count (INTF, c. 2023) |

Add to this approximately 10,000 Latin Vulgate manuscripts, and thousands more in Syriac, Coptic, Armenian, Ethiopic, Georgian, and Arabic — totaling roughly 24,000+ manuscripts across all languages. <Footnote id="metzger-text" source="Metzger & Ehrman, *The Text of the New Testament*, p. 126." />

## What Early Agreement Tells Us

One of the most important findings from the manuscript tradition is what scholars call "early agreement" — the tendency for manuscripts from different geographical regions and textual traditions to agree on the vast majority of the text.

When P75 (Egypt, c. 200 AD) closely agrees with Codex Vaticanus (provenance unknown, c. 325 AD), that agreement is not coincidental. It indicates both are faithfully transmitting an earlier common ancestor. And when that agreement extends to the papyri, which are independent of the later uncials, scholars can triangulate to the original with high confidence.

Kurt and Barbara Aland, in their definitive introduction to New Testament textual criticism, summarize the situation:

> "The reader…may be certain that the text of the New Testament has been reliably transmitted. The original text of the New Testament can be recovered, substantially, from the manuscripts." <Footnote id="aland-aland" source="Aland & Aland, *The Text of the New Testament*, 2nd ed. Eerdmans, 1989, p. 28." />

## Ongoing Discovery

The manuscript tradition is not closed. The CSNTM continues to photograph and catalogue previously unexamined manuscripts in monastery libraries across Greece, Turkey, Egypt, and elsewhere. In some cases, newly catalogued manuscripts provide fresh evidence for specific textual questions.

The story of New Testament manuscripts is, in a very real sense, still being written. But the broad outlines — extraordinary quantity, remarkable temporal proximity, widespread early agreement — have been established beyond serious dispute.
