# Manuscript Evidence

The New Testament is the best-attested ancient document in existence — by a wide margin. These are the numbers.

<ManuscriptChart client:visible />

<GapVisualization client:visible />

<ExpandSection label="Why these numbers are significant">

The official registry of Greek New Testament manuscripts, maintained by the INTF in Münster, currently lists over **5,800 Greek manuscripts**. <Footnote id="intf-manuscripts" source="Institut für Neutestamentliche Textforschung, *Kurzgefasste Liste der griechischen Handschriften des Neuen Testaments*. Available at ntvmr.uni-muenster.de." /> When manuscripts in Latin, Syriac, Coptic, Armenian, Ethiopic, and other languages are included, the total exceeds 24,000. <Footnote id="metzger-text" source="Metzger & Ehrman, *The Text of the New Testament*, 4th ed., p. 126." />

{/* VERIFY: Cross-check the 24,000 figure. Metzger (2005) uses ~24,000 for all languages. Wallace (CSNTM) has used 24,000+. The Greek-only count from INTF fluctuates as new manuscripts are identified; ~5,800 is the figure as of 2023. */}

For context, here's how the New Testament compares:

| Work | Manuscripts | Earliest Copy | Gap |
|------|-------------|---------------|-----|
| New Testament (Greek) | ~5,800 | ~125 AD (P52) | ~30–50 years |
| New Testament (all languages) | ~24,000+ | ~125 AD | ~30–50 years |
| Homer, *Iliad* | ~1,800 | ~200 BC | ~400 years |
| Caesar, *Gallic Wars* | ~251 | ~9th c. AD | ~900 years |
| Tacitus, *Annals* | ~33 | ~9th–11th c. AD | ~800–1,000 years |
| Herodotus, *Histories* | ~109 | ~900 AD | ~1,400 years |
| Plato | ~210 | ~895 AD | ~1,200 years |

{/* VERIFY: Manuscript counts for classical works vary by source. Figures above from Reynolds & Wilson, *Scribes and Scholars*; Metzger, *Manuscripts of the Greek Bible*. */}

The logic behind quantity is **triangulation**: multiple independent copies allow scholars to identify and correct scribal errors. More manuscripts means *better ability* to reconstruct the original — not more uncertainty. The common misunderstanding reverses this logic.

Bruce Metzger and Bart Ehrman write in their joint academic text:

> "The Greek manuscripts of the New Testament are a far richer resource than those of any other ancient literary work." — *The Text of the New Testament*, p. 51

Ehrman uses this richness to argue that we can *identify* variants. Metzger uses it to argue the text is *recoverable*. Both are right — and both are working from the same evidence.

</ExpandSection>

<ExpandSection label="Details on the most significant early manuscripts">

**P52** (Rylands Library Papyrus P52) — The earliest known NT fragment. Contains John 18:31–33, 37–38. Dated c. 100–150 AD, likely c. 125 AD. Held at the John Rylands Library, Manchester. <Footnote id="intf-manuscripts" source="INTF, Papyrus 52 entry. Digital facsimile available at digital.library.manchester.ac.uk." />

**P66** (Bodmer Papyri II) — Contains most of the Gospel of John. Dated c. 200 AD. Held at the Bodmer Library, Geneva.

**P75** (Bodmer Papyri XIV–XV) — Contains significant portions of Luke and John. Dated c. 175–225 AD. Now held at the Vatican Apostolic Library; available in high-resolution digital facsimile.

**P46** (Chester Beatty Papyrus II) — Contains most of Paul's letters. Dated c. 175–225 AD. Split between the Chester Beatty Library in Dublin and the University of Michigan.

**Codex Sinaiticus** — Complete New Testament plus portions of the Old Testament and early Christian writings. Dated c. 330–360 AD. Originally discovered at St. Catherine's Monastery, Sinai; now divided among the British Library, Leipzig University Library, the National Library of Russia, and St. Catherine's Monastery. [Fully digitized at codexsinaiticus.org](https://www.codexsinaiticus.org/).

**What the Church Fathers add:** By the late second and early third century, writers like Clement of Alexandria, Origen, Tertullian, and Irenaeus were quoting extensively from what we recognize as the New Testament. Scholars have observed that if every NT manuscript were destroyed, the text could be reconstructed — with very few gaps — from patristic quotations alone. <Footnote id="holmes-apostolic" source="Holmes, *The Apostolic Fathers*, 3rd ed. Baker Academic, 2007." />
