# Old Testament Manuscripts

The manuscript evidence for the Old Testament (Hebrew Bible) is different in character from that of the New Testament, but its story is, if anything, more dramatic — because the defining chapter of that story was unwritten until 1947.

## Before 1947: The Masoretic Text

For most of modern history, our oldest complete manuscripts of the Hebrew Bible dated to approximately the 10th–11th century AD. The primary text tradition, known as the **Masoretic Text** (MT), was preserved and transmitted by Jewish scholars called the Masoretes beginning around the 6th century AD.

The Masoretes were meticulous copyists. They developed elaborate systems of verification — counting letters, words, and verses per book; noting the middle letter and middle word of the Torah; recording marginal notations about unusual spellings. Their goal was zero-defect transmission, and their methods reflected a theology of textual sanctity.

The oldest complete Hebrew Bible manuscript in the Masoretic tradition is the **Aleppo Codex**, produced c. 925 AD and housed at the Israel Museum, Jerusalem. The **Leningrad Codex** (St. Petersburg), dated 1008–1009 AD, is the oldest *complete* Hebrew Bible manuscript and forms the basis for most modern critical editions.

For most of the 20th century, this meant our best Hebrew text witnesses dated from roughly 1,000 years after the books were composed — a significant gap, comparable to the gaps we noted for classical Greek works.

Then the shepherds found the caves.

## The Dead Sea Scrolls

In 1947, a Bedouin shepherd named Muhammad edh-Dhib discovered jars containing ancient scrolls in a cave near the Dead Sea at Qumran. The resulting excavations between 1947 and 1956 uncovered approximately 900–1,000 documents (many fragmentary) in eleven caves, including manuscripts of every book of the Hebrew Bible except Esther. <Footnote id="abegg-flint" source="Abegg, Flint & Ulrich, *The Dead Sea Scrolls Bible*. HarperSanFrancisco, 1999, p. xv." />

These manuscripts dated from approximately 250 BC to 68 AD — pushing our manuscript evidence for the Hebrew Bible back by roughly a **thousand years**.

The academic custodianship of the scrolls is complex: the Israel Antiquities Authority oversees them, many are housed at the Israel Museum's Shrine of the Book in Jerusalem, and the Israel Museum has made high-resolution digital images freely available through their [Digital Dead Sea Scrolls project](https://www.deadseascrolls.org.il/). <Footnote id="dss-digital" source="Israel Museum, The Digital Dead Sea Scrolls. deadseascrolls.org.il. The project makes high-resolution images of key scrolls freely accessible." />

### What the Scrolls Tell Us

The Dead Sea Scrolls included multiple copies of many biblical books. The most complete is **1QIsa^a** (the Great Isaiah Scroll), a nearly complete copy of Isaiah dating to approximately 125 BC.

When scholars compared 1QIsa^a with the Masoretic Text of Isaiah — documents separated by approximately 1,000 years of copying — the result was striking:

Emanuel Tov, the lead scholar on the Dead Sea Scrolls publication project and Professor Emeritus at Hebrew University, describes the relationship this way:

> "The textual tradition behind the Masoretic Text was very stable during the Second Temple period, and the Masoretic Text, as we know it from medieval manuscripts, represents an ancient tradition that was already established in the first century BCE or earlier." <Footnote id="tov-textual" source="Tov, *Textual Criticism of the Hebrew Bible*, 3rd ed. Fortress Press, 2012, p. 28." />

For Isaiah specifically, the two texts agree closely on the vast majority of the text. Where differences exist, most are minor orthographic variations (fuller vs. shorter spellings, which reflect scribal conventions rather than different readings), a handful of variant readings, and a few passages where the MT and DSS diverge more significantly. <Footnote id="tov-scribal" source="Tov, *Scribal Practices and Approaches*, Brill, 2004." />

This does not mean there are no differences — there are. But the degree of agreement across a millennium of copying is remarkable by any standard of ancient documentary transmission.

## The Septuagint

The Old Testament manuscript tradition also includes the **Septuagint** (LXX), a Greek translation of the Hebrew Bible produced in Alexandria beginning around 250 BC. The Septuagint is significant for several reasons:

1. **Age**: Our manuscripts of the Septuagint (including portions in the Dead Sea Scrolls) predate many Hebrew manuscripts.
2. **Usage**: The New Testament quotes the Old Testament almost entirely from the Septuagint, not the Hebrew, making the LXX the Bible of the first Christians.
3. **Variants**: In some books (especially Jeremiah, Ezekiel, and Daniel), the Septuagint differs significantly from the Masoretic Text — a difference partially explained by the Dead Sea Scrolls, which show that multiple Hebrew text traditions circulated in the Second Temple period.

This complexity is not a weakness but a strength of honest scholarship: the textual tradition of the Hebrew Bible is nuanced, and scholars like Emanuel Tov have spent careers carefully mapping it.

## Conclusion

The Old Testament manuscript tradition, enriched immeasurably by the Dead Sea Scrolls, gives us genuine confidence in the stability of the Hebrew text. The Masoretic tradition, once standing alone with a 1,000-year gap, now has first-century BC corroboration — and the agreement between the two is a testament to the meticulous work of generations of Jewish scribes for whom the text was sacred.

This does not resolve every textual question; scholars continue to work through the variants and their implications. But the broad outline of a stable, carefully transmitted text is well-established.
