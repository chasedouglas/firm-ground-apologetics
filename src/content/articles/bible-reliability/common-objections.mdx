# Common Objections

This page addresses the five objections most commonly raised about Bible reliability in popular conversation. We try to state each objection in its strongest form — not a strawman — before responding.

---

## Objection 1: "The Bible has been translated so many times — it's like a game of telephone."

**The steelman**: Languages evolve, translators make choices, and each generation of translation introduces potential errors. By the time you get to a modern English Bible, you've gone through Latin, then German or English Reformation-era translations, then modern revisions. How can you trust something that far removed from the originals?

**The response**: This objection conflates *translation* with *transmission*, and it misunderstands how modern Bible translation works.

Modern English Bibles — the ESV, NIV, NASB, NET Bible, and others — are translated **directly from the original languages**: Hebrew and Aramaic for the Old Testament, Greek for the New Testament. The translators work not from a previous English translation but from critical editions of the Hebrew Masoretic Text (Biblia Hebraica Stuttgartensia or the newer Biblia Hebraica Quinta) and the Greek New Testament (Nestle-Aland 28th edition or the UBS 5th edition). <Footnote id="aland-aland" source="Aland & Aland, *The Text of the New Testament*, 2nd ed. The NA28 and UBS5 are the standard critical editions used in seminary and academic translation." />

The "telephone game" image suggests each translation is made from the previous translation. This was true of the KJV-era Bibles for some purposes (Jerome translated to Latin from Greek; later translators used Jerome's Vulgate as a reference), but modern scholarship returned to the Greek and Hebrew originals in the 19th–20th centuries, and all major modern translations go directly to those sources.

Translation is *interpretation* — there is genuine scholarly disagreement about how to render certain Greek or Hebrew idioms — but it is not a compounding chain of errors. Every modern translator is one step from the reconstructed original text.

---

## Objection 2: "There are hundreds of thousands of errors in the manuscripts."

**The steelman**: Bart Ehrman, a prominent New Testament scholar, says there are more variants in the manuscripts than there are words in the New Testament. That's an astonishing number of errors. How can we possibly know what the original said?

**The response**: The number is real. The context matters enormously.

Ehrman himself — whose credibility on this point is unimpeachable to skeptics — writes: "Most of these differences are completely immaterial and unimportant and don't matter for anything other than to show that scribes could not spell any better than most people can today." <Footnote id="ehrman-misquoting" source="Ehrman, *Misquoting Jesus*, p. 10." />

The variant count grows with manuscript quantity. We have approximately 5,800 Greek New Testament manuscripts. A spelling variant that appears in 500 manuscripts is counted as 500 variants. The large variant count is partly a function of how richly attested the New Testament is — not just how corrupted it is.

Textual critics classify variants:
- **~75–80%** are spelling/orthographic differences (the Greek equivalent of "color" vs. "colour")
- **~10–15%** are word-order differences (usually inconsequential in Greek)
- **~5–10%** are synonymous word substitutions
- **~1–2%** are meaningful-and-viable variants — places where the text might actually mean something different depending on which reading is original

Ehrman and evangelical textual critics like Daniel Wallace agree on these proportions. Their disagreement is theological and epistemological: is the recovered text good enough for religious confidence? That is a legitimate question, but it is a different question from whether we can know what the originals said.

## Objection 3: "The canon was decided at the Council of Nicaea."

**The steelman**: Constantine convened the Council of Nicaea and used it to consolidate his power and standardize Christianity. The books that supported the official version were "in"; books that were inconvenient were left out. The whole process was political.

**The response**: The Council of Nicaea (325 AD) did not address the canon of Scripture. There is no record in any document from the Council — including the accounts of those present — of a vote or decision on which books belonged in the Bible. <Footnote id="bruce-canon" source="Bruce, *The Canon of Scripture*, pp. 97–99." />

This claim appears to derive from a misreading of a much later account (Voltaire may have originated the modern popular version), and it has been corrected by historians of early Christianity across the theological spectrum — including non-Christian scholars.

The canon emerged through a gradual process of recognition based on apostolicity, catholicity (universal usage), and consistency with the apostolic teaching. By the late 2nd century — 150 years before Nicaea — Irenaeus of Lyon was treating the four Gospels as a settled collection. The core Pauline letters were recognized even earlier (2 Peter 3:16 refers to Paul's letters as "Scripture").

Councils did eventually produce formal canon lists — but the councils of Hippo (393 AD) and Carthage (397 AD), not Nicaea, and those councils were ratifying what the church had already been using, not making new decisions.

## Objection 4: "The Gnostic gospels were suppressed because they contained uncomfortable truths."

**The steelman**: There were many gospels in early Christianity. The powerful church chose the ones that supported its power and suppressed the others. The Gnostic gospels — Gospel of Thomas, Gospel of Philip, Gospel of Mary — present a very different Jesus. Their exclusion was political.

**The response**: The Gnostic gospels were not "suppressed" in any meaningful sense. They were read, discussed, debated, and rejected — publicly, by early Christian writers who quoted extensively from them. Irenaeus of Lyon spent considerable space in *Adversus Haereses* (~180 AD) describing and refuting Gnostic teachings. You cannot suppress something by writing a 5-volume rebuttal of it. <Footnote id="bruce-canon" source="Bruce, pp. 248–261." />

More importantly, the Gnostic gospels were not rejected because they were "politically inconvenient." They were rejected because their content reflected a quite different theology — one centered on secret spiritual knowledge, a wholly spiritual savior, and a rejection of the Jewish scriptures and the goodness of creation — that was not continuous with the apostolic teaching about the Jewish Messiah Jesus of Nazareth.

The "suppressed truths" narrative also misrepresents the Gnostic texts. They do not present Jesus as a human revolutionary with a wife, as Dan Brown's fiction suggested. They present a docetic or Gnostic Christ who is barely human, who speaks in riddles about escaping the material world, and whose community is defined by esoteric initiation. Elaine Pagels, sympathetic to the Gnostic writings, describes them on their own terms — not as suppressed historical accounts of a more human Jesus. <Footnote id="ehrman-misquoting" source="Ehrman, *Lost Christianities*, pp. 52–78, covers the Gnostic texts with appropriate nuance." />

## Objection 5: "It was written centuries after the events."

**The steelman**: The Gospels were written decades after Jesus died. The authors were not eyewitnesses. Oral tradition is unreliable. By the time things were written down, the story had already been significantly altered through transmission and theological development.

**The response**: This objection contains some valid points and some significant overclaims.

**What is valid**: The Gospels were not written as events occurred. Mark, the earliest Gospel, is typically dated to approximately 65–70 AD — roughly 35–40 years after the crucifixion. Luke and Matthew are typically dated to 80–90 AD; John to 90–100 AD. The authors were not live-tweeting events. <Footnote id="metzger-text" source="Metzger & Ehrman, *The Text of the New Testament*, pp. 25–26. Dates of composition are discussed in standard introductions; the ranges given reflect mainline critical scholarship." />

**What is overclaimed**: Several important considerations complicate the "written too late to be reliable" argument:

*Paul's letters predate the Gospels.* Paul's letters — universally accepted as authentic by critical scholars — date to the 50s AD. Paul writes in 1 Corinthians 15:3–8 of a creedal tradition he received and passed on, describing resurrection appearances. Most scholars date this creed to within 3–7 years of the crucifixion. This is documentary evidence well within the living memory of witnesses.

*Oral tradition in ancient cultures was more reliable than modern assumptions suggest.* Ancient Jewish culture placed high value on accurate oral transmission. Studies of oral tradition in similar cultures indicate that core traditions were transmitted with care by designated tradents. This does not mean oral tradition was perfect, but the "telephone game" caricature is not accurate to how ancient oral transmission actually worked.

*The gap to the earliest manuscripts is measured in decades, not centuries.* As noted in the manuscript evidence section, P52 dates to roughly 125 AD — within a generation of the Gospel of John's composition. The text was being copied very early.

*Luke explicitly claims to have investigated carefully.* Luke 1:1–4 describes an explicit historiographic methodology: consulting eyewitnesses and carefully investigating everything. Whether Luke's execution matches his stated methodology is a separate historical question, but the claim to eyewitness investigation is there in the text.

The dating question is genuinely important, and historians debate it seriously. But "written centuries after events" — implying a gap of hundreds of years — is significantly overstated for the New Testament documents.

---

These objections are worth taking seriously. None of them, when examined closely, provides a decisive reason to distrust the textual reliability of the New Testament. They raise legitimate questions that deserve honest engagement — which we have tried to provide.
