<ExpandSection label="Why do manuscript counts and gaps matter?">

When evaluating any ancient text, historians care about two things: **how many independent copies survive**, and **how close in time those copies are to the original**.

More copies mean you can cross-check scribal errors. When a scribe in Egypt makes a mistake copying a text, and a scribe in Syria independently doesn't, comparing those two traditions lets scholars identify and correct the error. With only one copy, you have no way to know what was changed.

Temporal proximity matters because errors and deliberate changes compound over time. A copy made 30 years after the original is far more likely to reflect the original accurately than one made 1,000 years later, where each generation of copies introduced new variations.

On both measures, the New Testament stands apart from every other work of comparable antiquity — not by a small margin, but by an order of magnitude.

**Bart Ehrman** — the most prominent popular-level skeptic of the New Testament — acknowledges this directly:

> "In spite of the remarkable differences among our manuscripts, scholars are convinced that we can reconstruct the original words of the New Testament with reasonable (though not perfect) accuracy." — *Misquoting Jesus*, p. 62

Notice what he's *not* saying. He's not claiming the text is hopelessly corrupt. His argument is about the *interpretation* of variants — not their volume or severity. Both Ehrman and conservative scholars like Bruce Metzger work from the same manuscript evidence. Their disagreement is over conclusions, not data.

**What textual reliability does and does not prove:** Demonstrating that our manuscripts faithfully transmit the first-century text does *not* prove the authors were accurate eyewitnesses, or that miracles happened. Those are separate questions. What it does establish is that we're reading substantially what the first-century authors wrote — not a text corrupted by centuries of ecclesiastical editing.
