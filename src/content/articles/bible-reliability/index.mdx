# Bible Reliability: An Overview

When people ask whether the Bible is reliable, they are often asking several different questions at once — and conflating them makes the conversation go nowhere fast.

**Is the text we have today faithful to the original manuscripts?** This is a question of *textual transmission*, and it is answered by manuscript evidence, the discipline of textual criticism, and the history of translation.

**Did the authors accurately describe what they witnessed or researched?** This is a question of *historical reliability*, and it involves archaeology, external corroboration, and the dating of documents.

**Does the text say what it claims to say, and do those claims hold up?** This is a question of *interpretive and theological reliability*, which involves exegesis, hermeneutics, and philosophy.

This section addresses the first question almost exclusively: *do the documents we hold today faithfully represent the earliest texts?* The answer, as we will see, is more confident than most people expect — and the evidence comes not primarily from Christian apologists, but from the mainstream academic institutions that study ancient manuscripts.

## Why This Question Matters

It is sometimes assumed that skepticism about the Bible requires denying its textual transmission. But this gets the logic backwards. Bart Ehrman — arguably the most prominent popular-level skeptic of the New Testament — acknowledges that we can reconstruct the original text of the New Testament to a remarkable degree:

> "In spite of the remarkable differences among our manuscripts, scholars are convinced that we can reconstruct the original words of the New Testament with reasonable (though not perfect) accuracy." <Footnote id="ehrman-misquoting" source="Ehrman, Bart D. *Misquoting Jesus*. HarperOne, 2005, p. 62." />

Notice what Ehrman is *not* saying. He is not saying the text is hopelessly corrupt. He is saying that the sheer volume of variants — inevitable in any hand-copied tradition — means we cannot always be certain about every word. That is a very different claim, and it is one that mainstream textual scholars across the theological spectrum largely accept.

The question of textual reliability is, in other words, not a battle between believers and skeptics. It is an empirical question with a fairly clear answer: the New Testament is the best-attested ancient document in existence, by a wide margin.

## The Evidence at a Glance

The two primary metrics for evaluating ancient textual transmission are:

1. **Manuscript quantity**: How many hand-copied manuscripts survive?
2. **Manuscript proximity**: How close in time is the earliest surviving copy to the original composition?

On both measures, the New Testament stands apart from every other ancient work.

## What Textual Reliability Does and Does Not Prove

A common mistake — made by both overeager apologists and dismissive skeptics — is to confuse *textual reliability* with *historical accuracy* or *theological truth*. They are distinct questions.

Demonstrating that our New Testament manuscripts faithfully transmit what was written in the first century does **not** demonstrate that the authors were accurate eyewitnesses, that miracles happened, or that Jesus rose from the dead. Those questions require different kinds of evidence.

What textual reliability *does* establish is the proper foundation for all further inquiry: we are reading substantially what the first-century authors wrote. The text has not been "changed over centuries" in any meaningful sense — certainly not in the way popular culture imagines, with powerful church councils quietly editing embarrassing passages.
