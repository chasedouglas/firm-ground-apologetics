# Textual Criticism

There are 200,000–400,000 variants across New Testament manuscripts. Here's what that actually means — and what both Ehrman and Wallace agree on.

<TextualVariantExplorer client:visible />

<ExpandSection label="What a variant is and how they're classified">

A **textual variant** is any place where manuscripts differ from one another — a different spelling, a word omitted, two words swapped. They accumulate in any hand-copied tradition. Over centuries of copying, they add up.

The number 200,000–400,000 sounds alarming. Context is everything:

**~75–80%: Spelling and nonsense variants.** The Greek equivalent of "honor" vs. "honour," or unintentional misspellings. Zero effect on meaning.

**~10–15%: Word-order variants.** Greek syntax is flexible. "Jesus loves John" and "John Jesus loves" are both valid Greek constructions communicating the same fact.

**~5–10%: Synonymous variants.** A scribe substituted "Lord" for "Jesus," or vice versa. Meaningful to historians studying scribal habits, but rarely affect the substance of a passage.

**~1–2%: Meaningful and viable variants.** These affect meaning *and* could plausibly represent the original reading. This is where serious textual study happens. Even within this category, the vast majority do not affect any doctrine that isn't taught elsewhere in the New Testament.

Bart Ehrman — whose *Misquoting Jesus* is the most widely read skeptical treatment of this subject — writes in that same book:

> "It would be a mistake…to think that there are thousands of places in which the meaning of the text is in doubt… In fact, the most significant textual variants affect places that affect no important Christian doctrine." — *Misquoting Jesus*, pp. 252–253 <Footnote id="ehrman-misquoting" source="Ehrman, *Misquoting Jesus*, pp. 252–253." />

Daniel Wallace (conservative, Dallas Theological Seminary) agrees with Ehrman on the raw data. Their disagreement is whether a text recovered to very high confidence is sufficient for religious trust — a theological question, not a textual one.

</ExpandSection>

<ExpandSection label="How modern textual criticism works (the CBGM)">

Modern NT textual criticism now uses computational methods. The **Coherence-Based Genealogical Method** (CBGM), developed at the INTF Münster, analyzes manuscript relationships algorithmically to reconstruct the likely family tree of the tradition. <Footnote id="wasserman-gurry" source="Wasserman & Gurry, *A New Approach to Textual Criticism*. SBL Press, 2017." /> It now underlies the *Editio Critica Maior* — the critical edition of the Greek New Testament.

The result is a more rigorous, computationally grounded text than older 20th-century editions. The changes from previous editions are, characteristically, minor.

Textual criticism is not the enemy of confidence in the NT text — it is the *source* of that confidence. By rigorously analyzing variants, scholars across the theological spectrum have reconstructed a text they believe represents what the original authors wrote to a very high degree.

</ExpandSection>
