# The Dead Sea Scrolls

In 1947, scholars had manuscripts of Isaiah dating to ~1,000 AD. The Dead Sea Scrolls pushed that back to ~125 BC — 1,125 years earlier. Here's what they found when they compared them.

<DeadSeaScrollsSlider client:visible />

<ExpandSection label="What the comparison means, and what it doesn't">

The Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsa^a) is a nearly complete copy of Isaiah, running 54 columns on 17 sheets of parchment stitched together — 7.3 meters (24 feet) unrolled. Paleographic analysis and radiocarbon dating place it at approximately **125 BC**. <Footnote id="tov-textual" source="Tov, *Textual Criticism of the Hebrew Bible*, 3rd ed. Fortress, 2012, pp. 102–106." />

**Isaiah 53 specifically:** The messianic "Suffering Servant" passage shows remarkable agreement. Of the 166 Hebrew words in Isaiah 53, the Great Isaiah Scroll differs from the Masoretic Text in only 17 letters — 11 are minor spelling differences, 4 are stylistic differences, and only 2 involve potentially meaningful words, neither of which changes the passage's substance. <Footnote id="vanderkam-flint" source="VanderKam & Flint, *The Meaning of the Dead Sea Scrolls*, p. 133." />

{/* VERIFY: The "17 letters / 166 words" statistic for Isaiah 53 is widely cited in apologetics contexts. Verify against Abegg-Flint-Ulrich or Tov's apparatus. The substance (remarkable agreement) is not disputed; the specific count should be verified. */}

**What the comparison yields overall:**

- **Orthographic variations**: The most common differences are spelling. The Scroll uses "fuller" spellings — adding extra letters later dropped in the Masoretic tradition. Zero effect on meaning.
- **Minor word variations**: In some places, a word is added, omitted, or substituted. Emanuel Tov — the leading authority on DSS textual criticism — notes the vast majority are trivial. <Footnote id="abegg-flint" source="Abegg, Flint & Ulrich, *The Dead Sea Scrolls Bible*, pp. xxiv–xxv." />
- **LXX-aligned variants**: A small number align with the Septuagint (Greek translation) rather than the MT, showing that multiple textual traditions circulated in the Second Temple period.

The honest summary: *the essential content of the Hebrew Bible was stable*, while *the precise textual form was not absolutely uniform* in the Second Temple period. Both are true.

For Isaiah 53 specifically: whatever one's interpretation, the text existed in its essential form centuries before Jesus was born.

</ExpandSection>

<ExpandSection label="The discovery and what else the Scrolls contain">

In early 1947, a Bedouin shepherd searching for a lost goat near Qumran tossed a stone into a cave and heard breaking pottery. Inside: clay jars with leather scrolls wrapped in linen.

By 1956, eleven caves had yielded approximately 900–1,000 documents (many fragmented), dated from roughly 250 BC to 68 AD. <Footnote id="vanderkam-flint" source="VanderKam & Flint, *The Meaning of the Dead Sea Scrolls*, pp. 3–11." /> Fragments of every Hebrew Bible book were found — except Esther.

**Other notable findings:**

- **Psalms**: Over 40 manuscripts; general high agreement with the MT, though Psalm order differs in some scrolls.
- **Deuteronomy**: Second most attested book; some align with MT, some with the Samaritan Pentateuch or LXX.
- **Jeremiah**: Copies aligning with the shorter LXX Jeremiah confirmed that the LXX wasn't translating capriciously — multiple Jeremiah texts existed in antiquity.

The Scrolls were deposited *before* the standardization of the Hebrew text (roughly 70–200 AD), which is why they sometimes reflect earlier variant forms. The standardization that followed is what became the Masoretic tradition.

The Israel Museum has made high-resolution digital scans freely available at the [Digital Dead Sea Scrolls project](https://www.deadseascrolls.org.il/). <Footnote id="dss-digital" source="Israel Museum, The Digital Dead Sea Scrolls. Fully accessible at deadseascrolls.org.il." />

</ExpandSection>
