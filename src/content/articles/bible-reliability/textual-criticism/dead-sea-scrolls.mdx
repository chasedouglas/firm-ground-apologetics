# The Dead Sea Scrolls

The discovery of the Dead Sea Scrolls is one of the most significant events in the history of biblical scholarship. Before 1947, the oldest manuscripts of most Hebrew Bible books dated to around the 10th century AD — more than a millennium after the texts were composed. The Scrolls changed everything.

## The Discovery

In early 1947, a Bedouin shepherd named Muhammad edh-Dhib, searching for a lost goat near Qumran on the northwest shore of the Dead Sea, tossed a stone into a cave and heard the sound of breaking pottery. Inside, he found clay jars containing leather scrolls wrapped in linen.

The discovery triggered one of the largest archaeological excavations in history. By 1956, eleven caves near Qumran had yielded approximately 900–1,000 documents (many fragmented), dating from roughly 250 BC to 68 AD. <Footnote id="vanderkam-flint" source="VanderKam & Flint, *The Meaning of the Dead Sea Scrolls*, HarperSanFrancisco, 2002, pp. 3–11." />

Among these documents were fragments or scrolls of every book of the Hebrew Bible except Esther. The most complete is the Great Isaiah Scroll.

## The Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsa^a)

**1QIsa^a** is a nearly complete copy of the book of Isaiah, running to 54 columns on 17 sheets of parchment sewn together. It measures approximately 7.3 meters (24 feet) in length when unrolled.

Paleographic analysis and radiocarbon dating place its composition at approximately **125 BC** — roughly contemporary with the second-century BC copyist. <Footnote id="tov-textual" source="Tov, *Textual Criticism of the Hebrew Bible*, 3rd ed. Fortress, 2012, pp. 102–106." />

The Masoretic Text of Isaiah — the standard text used in modern translations and the oldest version available before 1947 — dates to manuscripts from the 10th–11th century AD. The Great Isaiah Scroll is approximately **1,100 years older** than those manuscripts.

The Israel Museum has made high-resolution digital scans of 1QIsa^a freely available at the [Digital Dead Sea Scrolls project](https://www.deadseascrolls.org.il/explore-the-archive/scroll/X-1). <Footnote id="dss-digital" source="Israel Museum, The Digital Dead Sea Scrolls. Fully digitized and freely accessible at deadseascrolls.org.il." />

## Comparing the Scrolls to the Masoretic Text

When scholars compared the Great Isaiah Scroll to the Masoretic Text of Isaiah, the degree of agreement was remarkable for documents separated by a millennium of hand-copying.

The comparison yields several categories of difference:

**Orthographic variations**: The most common differences are in spelling. The Isaiah Scroll often uses "fuller" (plene) spellings — adding extra letters, particularly *waw* and *yod*, that were later dropped in the Masoretic tradition. These spelling differences do not affect meaning.

**Minor word variations**: In some places, a word is added, omitted, or substituted. For Isaiah as a whole, Emanuel Tov — the leading authority on Dead Sea Scrolls textual criticism — notes that the vast majority of these are trivial. <Footnote id="abegg-flint" source="Abegg, Flint & Ulrich, *The Dead Sea Scrolls Bible*, p. xxiv–xxv." />

**More substantial variants**: A relatively small number of variants involve meaningful differences. Some of these align the Dead Sea Scrolls text with the Septuagint (Greek translation) rather than the Masoretic Text, suggesting that multiple textual traditions were circulating in the Second Temple period.

**Isaiah 53 specifically**: The messianic "Suffering Servant" passage, Isaiah 53, shows remarkable agreement between the Great Isaiah Scroll and the Masoretic Text. Of the 166 Hebrew words in the passage, the Great Isaiah Scroll differs from the MT in only 17 letters, and of those: 11 are minor spelling differences, 4 are stylistic differences, and only 2 involve words that are even potentially meaningful. Neither of those two changes affects the substance of the passage. <Footnote id="vanderkam-flint" source="VanderKam & Flint, p. 133." />

## What Other Scrolls Reveal

Beyond Isaiah, the Scrolls provide manuscript evidence for most of the Hebrew Bible, though often fragmentary. Some patterns:

**Psalms**: The most attested book in the Scrolls with over 40 manuscripts. Agreement with the MT is generally high, though the order of some Psalms in certain scrolls differs.

**Deuteronomy**: Second most attested. The Scrolls include portions that agree closely with the MT and some that show affinity with the Samaritan Pentateuch or the LXX.

**Jeremiah**: Notably, the LXX Jeremiah is about one-eighth shorter than the MT Jeremiah, and the Dead Sea Scrolls include copies of Jeremiah that align with the shorter LXX version — confirming that multiple Jeremiah texts circulated and that the Septuagint was not simply translating from a different edition capriciously.

This complexity has led scholars to conclude that the Hebrew Bible went through a period of textual plurality, followed by a period of standardization toward what became the Masoretic tradition — roughly 70–200 AD. The DSS were deposited before that standardization, which is why they sometimes reflect earlier variant forms.

## Significance for Christian Apologetics

The significance of the Dead Sea Scrolls for Christian apologetics is often misconstrued. The main apologetic claim — that the Scrolls confirm the stability of the Masoretic text — is valid as far as it goes. The agreement between 1QIsa^a and the MT is striking.

But the honest claim is more nuanced: the Scrolls show that *the essential content of the Hebrew Bible was stable* while also revealing that *the precise textual form was not absolutely uniform* in the Second Temple period. Both of these things are true, and both are significant.

For Isaiah 53 in particular — a passage Christians have long read as a messianic prophecy describing a suffering servant who bears the sins of many — the Scrolls confirm that the passage existed in its essential form centuries before the birth of Jesus. Whatever one's interpretation of the passage, the text itself predates the New Testament era.

## Access and Further Research

The Israel Museum's [Digital Dead Sea Scrolls](https://www.deadseascrolls.org.il/) project offers high-resolution images of the major scrolls with transcription and translation. The Leon Levy Dead Sea Scrolls Digital Library, hosted by the Israel Antiquities Authority, has made over 900 fragments available in even higher resolution. The *Dead Sea Scrolls Bible* by Abegg, Flint, and Ulrich translates every biblical text found among the Scrolls and is the most accessible scholarly treatment for general readers. <Footnote id="abegg-flint" source="Abegg, Flint & Ulrich, *The Dead Sea Scrolls Bible*. HarperSanFrancisco, 1999." />
