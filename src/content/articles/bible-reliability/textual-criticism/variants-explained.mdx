# Variants Explained

The number most people encounter is 200,000–400,000 — the often-cited count of textual variants in the New Testament manuscript tradition. Without context, this number sounds alarming. With context, it becomes evidence of scholarly thoroughness.

This article explains the three passages most commonly cited by critics as evidence of scribal corruption: the ending of Mark, the pericope adulterae, and the Comma Johanneum. We address them head-on because honest engagement with hard cases is more credible than pretending they don't exist.

## The Count in Context

The 200,000–400,000 variant figure counts every difference between every manuscript. Since we have approximately 5,800 Greek New Testament manuscripts, a single spelling variant that appears in 500 manuscripts counts as 500 variants. The count grows with manuscript quantity — which means it is partly a function of how rich the evidence is, not just how corrupt the text is.

As Bart Ehrman himself notes: "Most of these differences are completely immaterial and unimportant and don't matter for anything other than to show that scribes could not spell any better than most people can today." <Footnote id="ehrman-misquoting" source="Ehrman, *Misquoting Jesus*, p. 10." />

Ehrman and conservative scholars like Daniel Wallace disagree about the theological implications of the variants. They do not disagree substantially about the data.

## Case Study 1: Mark 16:9–20

**The variant**: The Gospel of Mark in our two oldest complete manuscripts — Codex Sinaiticus (c. 330–360 AD) and Codex Vaticanus (c. 325 AD) — ends at verse 16:8. A shorter ending appears in a few later manuscripts. The familiar "long ending" (verses 9–20, describing resurrection appearances and the Great Commission) appears in the majority of later manuscripts, beginning in the 4th–5th centuries. <Footnote id="metzger-commentary" source="Metzger, *A Textual Commentary on the Greek New Testament*, 2nd ed., pp. 102–107." />

**The honest assessment**: Most contemporary New Testament scholars, including evangelical conservatives, agree that Mark 16:9–20 was not part of the original Gospel of Mark. The vocabulary, style, and content differ from the preceding chapters, and the early manuscript evidence is strongly against inclusion.

Modern translations universally note this in their footnotes. The ESV, NIV, NASB, and CSB all include the long ending but note that "the earliest manuscripts and some other ancient witnesses do not have these verses."

**What this does and doesn't mean**: The absence of the long ending from the original Mark does not remove any teaching unique to it from Christian theology. The resurrection is attested in all four Gospels, Paul's letters, and the early creed in 1 Corinthians 15 — all of which predate or are contemporaneous with Mark. The Great Commission appears in Matthew 28:18–20 without textual dispute.

It means the original ending of Mark either ended abruptly at 16:8 (which many scholars believe was intentional and rhetorically powerful) or the original ending was lost at an early stage and multiple scribes supplied different endings.

This is a real textual question, openly discussed. It illustrates that textual criticism functions honestly — identifying uncertainty rather than hiding it.

## Case Study 2: John 7:53–8:11 (*Pericope Adulterae*)

**The variant**: The famous passage in which a woman caught in adultery is brought to Jesus and he says "Let him who is without sin among you be the first to throw a stone at her" — is absent from the earliest and most reliable John manuscripts. <Footnote id="metzger-commentary" source="Metzger, *Textual Commentary*, pp. 187–189." />

It is absent from P66 and P75 (second/third century papyri), from Codex Sinaiticus, Codex Vaticanus, and most other early manuscripts. When it does appear in later manuscripts, it sometimes appears in different locations — after John 7:36, after John 21:25, or even in Luke's Gospel.

The vocabulary and style also differ from the surrounding Johannine material.

**The honest assessment**: Most New Testament scholars conclude the passage was not part of the original Gospel of John. This is the position of almost all critical editions and is noted in the footnotes of modern translations.

**What this does and doesn't mean**: Many scholars who conclude the pericope is not original to John nevertheless believe it likely preserves an authentic tradition about Jesus — a story that circulated in early Christianity and was eventually inserted into the manuscript tradition. The story is coherent with Jesus's character and teaching as attested elsewhere.

Theologically, no doctrine depends uniquely on this passage. The broader teaching about forgiveness, mercy, and the danger of self-righteousness is abundantly attested throughout the Gospels.

The passage illustrates the textual tradition's complexity without undermining confidence in the overall text. It also illustrates how transparent modern scholarship is about these questions.

## Case Study 3: 1 John 5:7 (*Comma Johanneum*)

**The variant**: The Comma Johanneum is the phrase "For there are three that bear witness in heaven: the Father, the Word, and the Holy Spirit, and these three are one" (KJV, 1 John 5:7). This explicit Trinitarian formulation is arguably the clearest such statement in the New Testament.

It is also, as most NT scholars acknowledge, not original to 1 John. <Footnote id="metzger-commentary" source="Metzger, *Textual Commentary*, pp. 647–649." />

The phrase appears in none of the early Greek manuscripts. It appears to have originated in Old Latin manuscripts and early Latin Christian writings, possibly as a theological marginal note that was eventually incorporated into the text. It did not appear in most Greek manuscripts until the 15th–16th century.

Erasmus famously omitted it from his first two editions of the Greek New Testament (1516, 1519), and when challenged, said he would include it if a Greek manuscript containing it were produced. One promptly appeared (almost certainly fabricated to order). Erasmus included it in his third edition (1522) but noted his doubts.

**The honest assessment**: The Comma Johanneum is not original to 1 John. This is the consensus of virtually all New Testament textual scholars, including those who hold to a high view of Scripture. It was produced or popularized in the Latin tradition and is not attested in reliable Greek manuscripts.

**What this does and doesn't mean**: The doctrine of the Trinity does not depend on this verse. It is extensively grounded throughout the New Testament — in the baptism of Jesus (Matthew 3:16–17), in John's prologue (John 1:1–14), in the High Priestly Prayer (John 17), in the Pauline letters, and in the consistent devotional practice of the earliest church documented by Larry Hurtado and others. <Footnote id="hurtado-lord" source="Hurtado, *Lord Jesus Christ*, Eerdmans, 2003." />

What the Comma Johanneum case illustrates is that textual criticism works in both directions: it can identify late insertions as well as preserve early readings. The fact that scholars identified and removed this spurious addition is evidence that the discipline is functioning honestly.

## The Upshot

Three of the most significant textual issues in the New Testament — openly discussed, carefully studied, and honestly reported in modern translations. In each case:

1. The issue is acknowledged, not hidden
2. The evidence points clearly in one direction
3. No fundamental Christian doctrine is uniquely dependent on the disputed passage
4. The underlying text, stripped of clearly late additions, remains stable

This is what honest textual scholarship looks like. It is not a faith-threatening exercise — it is the very process that gives us confidence in what remains.
