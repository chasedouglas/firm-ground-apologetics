# Methodology

This page describes how we make decisions about sourcing, what we include and exclude, and how to report errors or suggest improvements.

## Our Goal

We are trying to produce apologetics material that is **honest about the evidence**, fair to skeptics, and useful to both believers and non-believers. This is harder than it sounds.

Apologetics has a reputation for motivated reasoning — cherry-picking evidence that supports a predetermined conclusion. We have tried to build in structural checks against that tendency. The most important: we cite skeptics by name and represent their actual arguments, not strawmen.

## Source Hierarchy

We distinguish four categories of sources, marked in our citations:

**Primary Sources** — Original documents, archaeological reports, and institutional repositories. Examples: the INTF manuscript registry, the Israel Museum's Digital Dead Sea Scrolls, the Codex Sinaiticus Project. When we cite numbers (manuscript counts, dates), we trace them to primary sources wherever possible.

**Academic Secondary Sources** — Peer-reviewed scholarship from academic publishers (Oxford, Cambridge, Eerdmans, SBL Press, Brill, Fortress) and secular universities. Examples: Metzger & Ehrman's *The Text of the New Testament*, Tov's *Textual Criticism of the Hebrew Bible*. These are preferred over apologetic sources for factual claims.

**Skeptical Sources** — Academic or popular works arguing against traditional Christian positions. Examples: Ehrman's *Misquoting Jesus*, Dale Martin's Yale lectures. We cite these directly because intellectual honesty requires engaging the actual skeptical scholarship, not a paraphrase of it.

**Apologetic Sources** — Works by Christian scholars defending traditional positions. Examples: Wallace, Blomberg, Licona, Craig. These are appropriate for arguments and interpretations; we prefer primary and academic sources for raw data claims.

## What We Exclude

**KJV-Onlyism and Textus Receptus-Onlyism**: Claims that the Textus Receptus (the Greek text underlying the KJV) is uniquely preserved and all other textual traditions are corrupted. This is not mainstream textual scholarship; it is a fringe position that misrepresents the manuscript evidence.

**Fringe archaeology**: Claims about archaeological discoveries that have not been confirmed by mainstream academic institutions (IAA, ASOR, etc.).

**Inflated statistics without primary source verification**: We try to trace every number to a primary or peer-reviewed source. Where we cannot, we flag with {/* VERIFY */} in the source. You can report these if you find them.

**Quotations out of context**: We do not quote skeptics in ways that misrepresent their positions. If we quote Ehrman agreeing that the text is substantially recoverable, we also cite the context in which he says it and his broader argument.

## `{/* VERIFY */}` Comments

Throughout the articles, you will see {/* VERIFY */} comments where we were not confident about a specific statistic or claim. These are reminders to chase down the primary source. If you find a verified source for one of these, or if you find an error, please open an issue.

## How to Report Errors

This site is open source. To report a factual error:

1. Open an issue at the GitHub repository with the article URL, the specific claim, and what you believe to be the correct information with a source.
2. If you have a correction, pull requests are welcome.

We take corrections seriously. Getting the evidence right matters more than protecting any specific claim.

## What This Site Does Not Do

This site addresses *textual reliability* and *manuscript evidence*. It does not:

- Argue for biblical inerrancy (that is a theological position that goes beyond what textual evidence supports or refutes)
- Claim to prove the Resurrection (that is addressed in the Historical Jesus and Resurrection sections, not here)
- Dismiss or mock the skeptical position
- Pretend that all questions are settled

We are comfortable with uncertainty where it genuinely exists. Where the evidence is strong, we say so. Where it is mixed, we say that too.
